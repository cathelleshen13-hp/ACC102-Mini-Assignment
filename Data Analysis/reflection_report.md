## Analysis of Global Inflation Dynamics Post-COVID

This project focuses on an analytical issue with practical relevance and significance in the real world: How has the inflation trend evolved in major economies after the COVID-19 pandemic? Which macroeconomic factors have the strongest correlation with the inflation dynamics? The target user group includes economics students, policy researchers, and ordinary informed individuals who wish to systematically and data-based understand the inflation pattern during the post-pandemic period. The reason I chose this topic is that inflation is one of the most influential macroeconomic phenomena in the 2020s of the 21st century, having had a profound impact on global households, enterprises, and policy makers; at the same time, the data set used in this project has a rich multi-country and multi-variable structure, which can support meaningful comparative analysis.

The dataset used in this project is `global_inflation_post_covid.csv`, which contains monthly observations for nine economies — USA, EU, China, India, Japan, UK, Brazil, Canada, and Vietnam — from 2020 to 2024. The variables include inflation rate, interest rate, oil price, GDP growth, unemployment rate, M2 money supply, exchange rate, food price index, and supply chain pressure index. The combination of these variables enables me not only to analyze inflation as the outcome variable, but also to explore its core driving factors: energy costs, food prices, monetary policy, supply chain conditions, and exchange rate dynamics. I chose this dataset because it covers both advanced and emerging market economies, enabling me to reveal both general patterns and country-specific differences through comparative perspectives.

Python was used as the core analytical tool throughout the project. The analysis process began by loading data through a relative path to ensure that the analysis process can be replicated even after the code repository is cloned. Subsequently, the missing values were checked, and the data gaps were filled using the forward filling method by grouping by country. After cleaning, I constructed a series of visualisations: inflation trend line charts with COVID shock and surge period annotations, a country-by-year heatmap, dual-axis charts comparing inflation with interest rates for four key economies, oil and food price comparisons for the USA, a supply chain pressure heatmap and scatter plot, a Pearson correlation heatmap across all variables, peak inflation identification per country, and M2 money supply normalisation and growth analysis.

The main findings from the project are as follows. Initially, all nine economies experienced periods of high inflation in 2021–2023, with the peak occurring in 2022. This general trend reflects the combination of post-lockdown demand recovery, supply chain disruptions, and the energy price shock following the Russia-Ukraine conflict. Secondly, oil prices and food price indices show the strongest positive correlation with inflation across the dataset, demonstrating the direct transmission effect of energy and food costs to consumer prices. Thirdly, developed economies such as the USA, UK, and EU raised interest rates significantly since 2022, while China and Japan maintained relatively loose monetary policy, reflecting different inflation conditions and growth priorities among each economies. Fourthly, supply chain pressure peaked in 2021–2022 and returned to normal levels in 2023, which coincides with the timing of inflation slowdown in most economies. Fifthly, emerging markets including Brazil, India, and Vietnam showed higher average inflation and more volatile average inflation levels, reflecting their greater exposure to commodity price fluctuations and currency depreciation.

There are several limitations to this analysis. The dataset uses simulated/synthetic data for some variables, which may not fully accurately reflect the dynamic situations in the real world. Correlation does not imply causation. Multiple factors act simultaneously and interact with each other in complex ways. The M2 money supply data is presented in local currency units. Without standardization, it cannot be directly compared between different countries. The exchange rate variable represents different base currencies across countries, which limits direct horizontal comparisons between different countries. The analysis does not model lagged effects, which are important in monetary transmission. Country-level aggregation masks significant regional and sectoral variation within each economy.

Through this project, I learned that inflation is a phenomenon driven by multiple factors and cannot be explained by a single factor alone. Cross-country comparative analysis shows that although there are common driving factors for this round of global inflation surge, the timing, magnitude and policy responses of inflation in different countries are significantly different. From a technical perspective, I practised handling panel data structures, transforming grouped data, constructing dual-axis charts, and building a correlation heatmap that summarises relationships across nine variables simultaneously. I also recognized the importance of standardizing variables before cross-country comparisons, as raw M2 figures in different currencies are not directly comparable.
